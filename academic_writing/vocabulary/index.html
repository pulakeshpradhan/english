    Academic Vocabulary & Flow

    Formal vs. Informal Vocabulary

    Academic writing demands a formal tone. Words that are acceptable in speech often sound unprofessional in writing.

    Very Informal Words to Avoid

    Informal Word Formal Correction Example Corrected
    So Very, Extremely The movie was so good. The movie was very good.
    Just Only, Simply There are just three students. There are only three students.
    Way, A lot Many, Much He is way better. He is much better.
    Kind of Somewhat It kind of looks like X. It somewhat looks like X.
    Tons of Numerous, Abundant We collected tons of data. We collected numerous samples.
    Stuff, Things Variables, Factors, Elements We measured various stuff. We measured various parameters.

    Informal Words

    Informal Word Formal Correction Example Corrected
    Get Obtain, Receive I got the data. I obtained the data.
    Of course Clearly, Evidently Of course it rained. It evidently rained.
    Awesome Significant, Remarkable The results were awesome. The results were significant.
    Again and again Repeatedly We tested again and again. We tested repeatedly.
    Whole Entire The whole day. The entire day.
    All right, OK Acceptable, Satisfactory The model is OK. The model is acceptable.
    Big, Huge Substantial, Significant There was a big increase. There was a substantial increase.
    Pretty Rather, Quite The results were pretty clear. The results were quite clear.

    Somewhat Informal Words

    Informal Word Formal Correction Example Corrected
    Kids Children, Juveniles The kids participated. The children participated.
    Cheap Inexpensive, Cost-effective The method is cheap. The method is cost-effective.
    Hard Difficult, Challenging The exam was hard. The exam was challenging.
    Used to Accustomed to I am used to... I am accustomed to...
    On the other hand Conversely, By contrast On the other hand... Conversely, ...
    Got to Must, Need to I've got to finish. I must complete.
    Right / Wrong Correct / Incorrect The answer was right. The answer was correct.
    Ask Enquire / Inquire I want to ask... I would like to enquire...
    Show Demonstrate, Illustrate The graphs show... The graphs demonstrate...
    Look at Examine, Investigate We looked at the data. We examined the dataset.

    Reporting Verbs

    When citing previous research, "said" is rarely sufficient. Use reporting verbs to convey precise meaning and your stance toward the citation.

    Comprehensive Reporting Verb Table

    Category Verbs When to Use Example
    Neutral States, Notes, Observes, Describes, Discusses Standard attribution, no judgment "Smith (2020) states that climate change affects migration patterns."
    Tentative Suggests, Proposes, Implies, Indicates, Postulates Author is cautious or speculative "Jones (2019) suggests a potential link between urban density and pollution."
    Strong/Assertive Argues, Claims, Asserts, Maintains, Contends Author makes a forceful argument "Lee (2021) argues that policy reform is essential for sustainability."
    Evidence-Based Demonstrates, Shows, Reveals, Establishes, Confirms Backed by data/findings "The study demonstrates a 40% reduction in emissions."
    Critical Criticizes, Challenges, Disputes, Questions, Refutes Author disagrees or critiques "Brown et al. (2022) challenge the assumptions underlying the model."
    Agreement Supports, Validates, Corroborates, Endorses Author agrees with previous work "Our findings corroborate the hypothesis proposed by Chen (2018)."

    Verb Choice Matters

    Your choice of reporting verb signals your stance. "Smith claims" can sound skeptical, while "Smith demonstrates" sounds authoritative. Choose carefully!

    Eliminating Wordiness (Conciseness)

    Academic writing should be concise. Remove redundant phrases ("deadwood").

    Extensive Wordiness Table

    Wordy Phrase Precise Replacement Example
    Due to the fact that Because ~~Due to the fact that~~ Because precipitation increased...
    In order to To ~~In order to~~ To measure temperature...
    Has the capability to Can The sensor ~~has the capability to~~ can detect changes.
    Making a decision Deciding ~~Making a decision~~ Deciding on methodology...
    In the event of If ~~In the event of~~ If rainfall exceeds...
    At this point in time Now, Currently ~~At this point in time~~ Currently, the model...
    A large number of Many, Numerous ~~A large number of~~ Numerous participants...
    It is important to note that Note that, Notably ~~It is important to note that~~ Notably, the correlation...
    Despite the fact that Although, Though ~~Despite the fact that~~ Although the sample was small...
    Prior to Before ~~Prior to~~ Before the experiment...
    Subsequent to After ~~Subsequent to~~ After data collection...
    In the vicinity of Near ~~In the vicinity of~~ Near the coastline...
    For the purpose of For, To ~~For the purpose of~~ To improve accuracy...
    In light of the fact that Because, Since ~~In light of the fact that~~ Because the results varied...
    On the basis of Based on ~~On the basis of~~ Based on these findings...

    Quantifiers and Intensifiers

    Replace vague quantifiers with precise academic alternatives.

    Vague/Informal Academic Precision Example
    A few Several, A small number of ~~A few~~ Several studies examined...
    Some Certain, Particular ~~Some~~ Certain variables demonstrated...
    Lots of Numerous, Abundant ~~Lots of~~ Numerous factors contribute...
    Really Significantly, Considerably The temperature increased ~~really~~ significantly.
    Very Highly, Extremely ~~Very~~ Highly correlated variables...

    Latin Abbreviations

    These are common but must be used correctly.

    • e.g. (exempli gratia): "For example." Use inside parentheses.
    • Usage: "Many variables (e.g., age, height, weight) were measured."
    • Note: Always followed by a comma.
    • i.e. (id est): "That is" or "In other words." It clarifies, doesn't list examples.
    • Usage: "The solstice (i.e., the longest day) occurs in June."
    • Note: Provides a specific clarification, not additional examples.
    • et al. (et alia): "And others." Used for citations with multiple authors. Note the period after "al".
    • Usage: "Smith et al. (2020) found..."
    • Note: Use when citing 3+ authors after first full citation.
    • etc. (et cetera): "And so forth." Avoid in formal writing; use "and others" or a specific list instead.
    • Better: "...such as temperature, pressure, and humidity" instead of "...temp, pressure, etc."
    • viz. (videlicet): "Namely." Introduces a detailed specification.
    • Usage: "Two districts (viz., Pune and Nagpur) were selected."
    • cf. (confer): "Compare with."
    • Usage: "Urban growth patterns (cf. rural areas) show higher density."

    Transitional Words and Phrases

    Transitional words are the bridges between your ideas. They ensure logical flow and coherence.

    Comprehensive Transition Matrix

    Purpose Transitions Example
    Adding New Ideas In addition, Moreover, Furthermore, Additionally, Also, Besides "The model was accurate. Moreover, it remained stable under stress."
    Contrasting However, Nevertheless, Nonetheless, Conversely, By contrast, Despite this, On the contrary, Yet "The hypothesis predicted growth. However, the data showed decline."
    Cause & Effect Therefore, Consequently, As a result, Accordingly, Thus, Hence, For this reason "Rainfall increased sharply. Consequently, flood risk rose."
    Comparing/Similarity Likewise, Similarly, Correspondingly, In the same way, Equally "Urban areas showed growth. Similarly, suburban regions expanded."
    Introducing Examples For example, For instance, Specifically, Namely, To illustrate "Several variables influenced results. For instance, temperature varied by 15°C."
    Emphasizing Indeed, In fact, Notably, Particularly, Especially "The correlation was strong. Indeed, it exceeded 0.9."
    Concluding In conclusion, To summarize, In summary, Finally, Ultimately, In brief "In conclusion, the model validated the hypothesis."
    Sequence First, Second, Third, Subsequently, Next, Then, Finally "First, we collected samples. Subsequently, we analyzed them."
    Clarifying That is, In other words, Specifically, To clarify, To put it another way "The variable was unstable. In other words, it fluctuated erratically."

    Punctuation Rules for Transitions

    Transitional words usually follow a period or semicolon and are immediately followed by a comma.

    1. I like pizza. However, I don't like pepperoni.
    2. I like pizza; however, I don't like pepperoni.
    3. The data was collected in May. Subsequently, it was analyzed in June.

    Common Error

    Never connect two independent clauses with just a comma and a transition word. That creates a comma splice!

    ❌ Wrong: "The model failed, therefore, we revised it."

    ✅ Correct: "The model failed. Therefore, we revised it."

    Hedging Language (Academic Caution)

    Academic writing requires cautious language to avoid overgeneralization.

    Hedging Verbs and Adverbs

    Type Examples Usage
    Modal Verbs May, Might, Could, Would "The data might suggest a correlation."
    Tentative Verbs Suggest, Indicate, Appear, Seem, Tend to "The results appear to support the hypothesis."
    Adverbs Possibly, Probably, Perhaps, Potentially, Likely, Apparently "This factor possibly contributed to the outcome."
    Adjectives Possible, Probable, Potential, Likely "A possible explanation is climate variability."

    Examples: * ❌ Too Strong: "This study proves that urbanization causes flooding." * ✅ Properly Hedged: "This study suggests that urbanization may contribute to increased flooding risk."

    Writer's Checklist

    Before submitting your manuscript, check the following:

    • [ ] Subjects: Does every sentence have one clear subject and idea?
    • [ ] Tone: Have you purged "so", "just", "kids", and "get"?
    • [ ] Spelling: Checked for specific errors (e.g., dropping 'e' before -ing)?
    • [ ] Structure: Are transitions used to connect paragraphs logically?
    • [ ] Flow: Do sentences vary in length to avoid monotony, while remaining direct?
    • [ ] Reporting Verbs: Have you used precise, varied reporting verbs instead of "said" or "showed"?
    • [ ] Wordiness: Have you eliminated redundant phrases and replaced them with concise alternatives?
    • [ ] Hedging: Have you used appropriate cautious language where claims are not absolute?
    • [ ] Latin Abbreviations: Are e.g., i.e., and et al. used correctly with proper punctuation?
