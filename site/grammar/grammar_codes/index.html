    The GRAMMAR Codes

    1-S-1-I Protocol (Structure)

    The Universal Rule

    1-S-1-I: A sentence must have 1 Subject and 1 Idea.

    The Problem: "Subject Overload"

    A run-on sentence happens when you pack two subjects/ideas into one space without a divider.

    Bad Code: "I went to the beach yesterday, it was hot."

    Error: Two subjects (I and It) = Two sentences needed.

    The Trap: "The Comma Splice"

    Never glue two independent ideas with just a distinct comma. It is too weak to hold them.

    Bad Code: "I bought milk, it was expensive."

    The Fix Kit: P-S-C

    Use the P-S-C method to repair run-ons:

    1. P - Period (The Hard Stop):
      • Separate them completely.
      • Result: "The model is accurate. It predicts floods well."
    2. S - Semicolon (The Soft Link):
      • Link related ideas with a semicolon + transition.
      • Result: "The model is accurate; therefore, it predicts floods well."
    3. C - Conjunction (The Glue):
      • Use a comma + FANBOYS (For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So).
      • Result: "The model is accurate, so it predicts floods well."

    The S-W-A-P Matrix (Vocabulary)

    The Standard

    Rule: If you say it in a text message, SWAP it for the Academic Standard.

    Tier 1: The "Lazy Words" Swap

    These words are vague fillers. Swap them for precision.

    Informal (Delete This) Formal (Write This) The Upgrade Example
    So Extremely / Very "The results were extremely significant."
    Just Only / Simply "The study used only two variables."
    Way / A lot Many / Much "The error was much lower."
    Kind of Somewhat "The trend was somewhat linear."
    Big Significant "The difference was significant."
    Good Robust "The method proved robust."
    Bad Poor "The data quality was poor."

    Tier 2: The "Chatty Words" Swap

    These words sound like speech. Swap them for writing.

    Informal (Delete This) Formal (Write This) The Upgrade Example
    Get Obtain / Receive "We obtained the satellite data."
    Of course Clearly / Evidently "The pattern clearly indicates stress."
    Awesome Incredible "The accuracy was incredible."
    Again and Again Repeatedly "The test failed repeatedly."
    Whole Entire "The entire study area."
    Kids Children "The children were surveyed."
    Show Illustrate "Figure 1 illustrates the trend."
    Look at Examine "We examined the samples."
    Talk about Discuss "This section discusses the results."

    Tier 3: The "Weak Links" Swap

    These phrases dilute your authority.

    Informal (Delete This) Formal (Write This) The Upgrade Example
    On the other hand Contrastingly "Contrastingly, the new method is faster."
    Got to Must / Need to "We must verify the data."
    Ask Enquire "We will enquire about the dataset."
    Used to Accustomed to "The system is accustomed to noise."
    In a nutshell Briefly "Briefly, the process involves..."
    Lots of Numerous "There are numerous factors."
    Sort of Partially "The hypothesis was partially supported."

    The B-R-I-D-G-E System (Transitions)

    The Flow Rule

    Rule: Never leave a sentence stranded. Build a BRIDGE to the next idea.

    Bridge Types

    Select the right bridge for your logic path:

    • (+) THE ADDER: Adds more of the same.
    • Code: In addition, Moreover, Furthermore.
    • (-) THE CONTRASTER: Changes direction.
    • Code: However, Conversely, Although, In contrast.
    • (=) THE RESULT: Shows the output.
    • Code: Therefore, Consequently, Thus, As a result.
    • (>) THE SEQUENCER: Shows order.
    • Code: First, Next, Finally.

    The Punctuation Key

    When using a Bridge Word, remember the Punctuation Code:

    [Punctuator] + [Bridge Word] + [Comma]

    • Option A: The test failed. However, we tried again.
    • Option B: The test failed; however, we tried again.

    The A-C-T-I-V-E Switch (Voice)

    The Agency Rule

    Rule: Focus on the Actor, not the Action. Active voice creates stronger, clearer sentences.

    The Passive Trap

    Passive voice makes sentences weak and wordy. It hides who is doing the work.

    Weak Code: "The survey was conducted by the research team."

    Critique: The actor (team) is at the end. It lacks energy.

    The Active Fix

    Flip the sentence. Put the actor first.

    Strong Code: "The research team conducted the survey."

    Why: It is direct, concise, and powerful.

    When to use Passive?

    Only use passive when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, or obvious.

    • Correct: "The samples were stored at -20Â°C." (Who stored them doesn't matter; the temperature matters).

    The P-A-R-A-L-L-E-L Rails (Consistency)

    The Pattern Rule

    Rule: Items in a list or comparison must follow the same grammatical pattern (noun-noun, verb-verb).

    The Mismatch Error

    Mixing verbs, nouns, and phrases breaks the flow and confuses the reader.

    Bad Code: "The goals are to reduce waste, increasing efficiency, and cost management."

    Error: Three distinct forms (Infinitive phrase, Gerund, Noun phrase).

    The Parallel Fix

    Make them match. Align the rails.

    Good Code: "The goals are to reduce waste, increase efficiency, and manage costs." (All Verbs).

    Alternative: "The goals are waste reduction, efficiency increase, and cost management." (All Nouns).
